THE ISOLATION OF ZURICH‚ 1520-1

On 10 August 1519 the plague broke out in Ziirich 3 Zwingli fell
a victim about the end of September (Opera, vii. 87, n. 1) and was
ill till the following March (ibid. 124). But he preached at inter-
vals3 and by 31 Dec. 1519 (ibid. 104) had won adherents, ‘plus
duobus millibus,' in spite of [No. 165] opposition both from the
three houses of Friars in the city and from the Chapter, 16 Feb.
1520 (ibid. 116). In 1520 he renounced his papal pension, ‘litteris
propria manu consignatis ⁹ (ibid. i. 365), and began to influence the
public action of the city: for [No. 166] the Town Council took
action in favour of purely Biblical preaching (ap. Ranke, Res.
515, n. 2). Next year, when Zwingli became, 29 April 1521,
Canon (Egli, Analecta Reformatoria, i. 22 s q.‚q) and thus citizen,
of Zurich, so effective was his [No. 167] preaching 111 opposition
to pensions and pensionaries (Bullinger, ap. Zumglzz' Opera, 11. ii.
350) that Francis I was forced to make alliance, 5 May 1521, 111111
1116 twelve Cantons only (Oechslif i, No. 116), while [No. 168]
Ziirich and its Communes [Gemeinden], 16-23 May 1521, repu-
diated the mercenary system (ibid. ii, No. 160). Thus [No. 169]
Ziirich was being isolated both in religion and politics, as
Zwingli wrote to his friend, Berchtold Haller, 1492—†1536, reformer
in Bern, 29 Dec. 1521 (Opera, vii. 187), before the crisis of reform
began.
